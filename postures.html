Introduction to Postures

The iconic crossed legged sitting position of meditation carries behind it great meaning but the origin of the meditation position is something very simple; this is how the practitioners were sat when they practiced meditation. Even though this makes your meditation position sound unimportant. An uncomfortable position versus a comfortable one makes all the difference on how your practice goes.

The Buddha recommended sitting in one of four positions for meditation. These are: standing, sitting, lying down and walking. Below we have described how it is best to implement 3 of these postures and their variations. Experiment with each posture until you find one that best serves you.

There are also a number of props that can be used to support the body for holding postures for a longer period of time with minimal effort and making it accesible to everybody.

What is important is that with each posture is that you find balance between being calm and alertness. Keeping the spine straight has been proven by science to help find this balance and mental clarity.

Conditioning

What is it?

Conditioning is an exercise that is good to practice before you start your meditation practice. It helps you arrive in the space, the breath, the posture and sometimes with an intention you set for your practice. It is a good way to prepare yourself to declare that you have set this time aside for yourself to practice your meditation.

Method

In your chosen meditation posture with the eyes open find a soft focus on an unmoving point. Take a big inhale through the nose and feel your abdomen expand; exhale fully through the mouth. Repeat twice and then let the breath return to normal. Gently close the eyes and relax the shoulders. Slightly tuck the chin to the chest and feel the rest of your body relax into the posture. Now begin your meditation practice.
