Introduction to Postures

The iconic crossed legged sitting position of meditation carries behind it great meaning but the origin of the meditation position is something very simple; this is how the practitioners were sat when they practiced meditation. Even though this makes your meditation position sound unimportant. An uncomfortable position versus a comfortable one makes all the difference on how your practice goes.

The Buddha recommended sitting in one of four positions for meditation. These are: standing, sitting, lying down and walking. Below we have described how it is best to implement 3 of these postures and their variations. Experiment with each posture until you find one that best serves you.

There are also a number of props that can be used to support the body for holding postures for a longer period of time with minimal effort and making it accessible to everybody.

What is important is that with each posture is that you find balance between being calm and alertness. Keeping the spine straight has been proven by science to help find this balance and mental clarity.

Conditioning

What is it?

Conditioning is an exercise that is good to practice before you start your meditation practice. It helps you arrive in the space, the breath, the posture and sometimes with an intention you set for your practice. It is a good way to prepare yourself to declare that you have set this time aside for yourself to practice your meditation.

Method

In your chosen meditation posture with the eyes open find a soft focus on an unmoving point. Take a big inhale through the nose and feel your abdomen expand; exhale fully through the mouth. Repeat twice and then let the breath return to normal. Gently close the eyes and relax the shoulders. Slightly tuck the chin to the chest and feel the rest of your body relax into the posture. Now begin your meditation practice.

Seated on the Floor

What is it?

The seated posture is one used in many meditation practices due to it keeping the practitioner balanced between calm and alert. It is a good starting position as props can be used to help make the position more comfortable.

Method

In this meditation posture you sit directly on the floor with legs crossed or kneeling. Keep the spine straight and the shoulders relaxed. Hands placed with the palms facing up resting on top of the knees or thighs. Try to keep the curve in the lower back from rounding as this will cause back pain.

Variations

Your knees should not be above the hips in this position, if they are you may need to sit on a cushion. You can sit with your back against a wall to start with a rolled up blanket at the curve of your lower back. You can also sit on a chair with your feet placed on the ground.

Lying Down

What is it?

A beneficial posture for those who find discomfort in a sitting posture even with the added props for support. This posture is also used in lengthy meditations where discomfort or pain after a long period of time is more likely. There is the risk of this posture leading to sleep during the meditation.

Method

Lying down on a comfortable surface let the legs extend long with the feet relaxed falling outwards. Have the arms extended along the sides of the body with the shoulder blades slightly tucked down the back so the palms are facing upwards.

Variations

If there is any lower back discomfort bend your knees and have your feet placed on the floor. You can also place a rolled up blanket underneath the knees with legs extended. Hands can be placed on the torso, palms facing down if having them on the floor is uncomfortable.

Standing

What is it?

Standing meditation is the most active of the postures and is a good choice if you struggle with staying awake during the lying down posture or you suffer pain in any sitting postures.

Method

It is better to do this posture without shoes on. Start by creating your foundation with your feet. Lift all 10 toes off the ground, spread them out and then release them back to the ground. Stand with your knees slightly bent. Roll the shoulders up towards the ears and down the back so they stay relaxed and your chest becomes open. Arms are dropped to the sides of the body with palms and elbow creases facing forwards. Slightly tuck the chin and the tailbone up towards your navel.

Variations

If standing becomes uncomfortable try walking whilst doing your meditation.

Props

What are they?

Props are useful pieces of equipment that can be invested in as your meditation practice grows to be used to help make your meditation posture more comfortable and lead to longer practices.

Types of Props

Sitting positions can be practiced on a bolster or cushion. There are specific cushions designed as meditation seats and other cushions that can be placed on the floor to make the contact with the knees on the ground more comfortable. Bolsters can be used to sit on in the kneeling position with the bolster placed lengthways between your legs as you sit on it. They can also be placed under the knees in the lying down position to alleviate any lower back discomfort by making it flat to the ground.
